🧵 A ₹7.9 Cr Flat in Byculla. A Dream that Ended in Court.

Here’s what every homebuyer must know about MahaRERA, contracts, and silence.

1️⃣ It Began With a Dream

Back in 2017, a couple booked a luxury 3BHK in Byculla East.

  • 📍 Flat No. 606
  • 📐 Carpet area: 1382.95 sq ft
  • 💰 Price: ₹6.75 Cr
  • 🧾 Booking amount: ₹10 lakh

2️⃣ A Changed Flat, A Changed Bill

Allotment in 2018 was for Flat No. 1006 on the 10th floor:

  • 📏 Carpet area: 1503.22 sq ft
  • 💸 Revised price: ₹7.90 Cr

The buyer didn’t formally object. That silence later proved costly.

3️⃣ The Hidden Cost of Silence

They signed the RFR without protest—giving the developer an upper hand later.

4️⃣ The Uneasy Build-Up

  • 🏗️ Height reduced
  • 🚧 More towers added
  • 🚗 Parking FSI absorbed
  • 📉 Amenities diluted

Buyers offered alternatives in 2021, but the builder refused to compromise.

5️⃣ The Disputed Deduction

Clause 4 of RFR allowed 10% forfeiture: ₹79.04 lakh. They had paid ₹1.58 Cr already.

So, they filed a complaint with MahaRERA in July 2022.

6️⃣ What the Buyers Argued

  • ⚖️ Unauthorized increase in area & cost
  • 📉 Layout changes without consent
  • 🚫 RERA Section 12 & 14 violations
  • 🔙 Refund with no forfeiture

7️⃣ MahaRERA's Verdict

  • ❌ No registered agreement
  • 🕑 Delayed objection = weak case
  • 📌 Forfeiture clause exceeded law

8️⃣ The Relief (and the Lesson)

  • ⚖️ Only 2% can be forfeited by law = ₹15.8 lakh
  • ✅ Refund: ₹1.42 Cr approx (excluding dues)

Builder has 45 days to comply.

9️⃣ The Core Takeaways

  • 🔍 Read every document
  • 🛑 Don’t ignore flat number, cost, area changes
  • 📑 Object in writing
  • 📅 Don’t delay filing complaints
  • ⚖️ A brochure ≠ a contract

🎬 In luxury towers, the real foundation is legal awareness—not just concrete.

Only the vigilant get justice.
